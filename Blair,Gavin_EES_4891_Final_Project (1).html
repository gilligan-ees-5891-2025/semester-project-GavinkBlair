Introduction

For a given power grid of buses (nodes) and transmission lines (edges), there exists an optimal power flow (OPF) which minimizes the cost of power generation. From this solution, there is a given amount of demand and a corresponding amount of power generated as a response. In analyzing this solution, a key assumption is made: there is a finite amount of power which may be produced by each generative bus. This prevents unreasonable optimization which would place the burden of generation on one or a handful of generators. OPF provides beneficial insights into real-time analysis of power grid operation. For this purpose, probabilistic methods which incorporate models of probability density functions (PDF) to represent load and ambient features such as wind speeds are implemented to provide some variability in the inputs of the optimization. This transforms grid operation from a deterministic study of control theory into an optimization problem with sets of outcomes to represent a range of worst-case or best-case scenarios. This informs grid operators of feature performance, not as binary failures or successes, but rather as probabilities from which to evaluate risk.

One of the key factors in quantifying risk in power grid operations is the requirement of a reserve margin in a set of generators. If a sudden spike in consumer demand (load) is present within the grid, the operators need to inject power into the grid accordingly, mandating that some generators produce energy at a specified range below capacity. This generation gap is considered the minimum reserve requirement (MRR). As a result of the incorporation of probability distributions from earlier, the probability that the MRR is violated for a given set of buses can be quantified and used to make decisions regarding future generator placement, further constraints on grid operation, and more.

To further discuss the methodology, we must first discuss three key types of buses for analysis: load buses, wind buses, and thermal buses. Load buses refer to stations of demand. Aggregated zonal demand is formed by summing all loads associated with buses in that zone. Wind buses refer to generators which rely on variable wind speed to contribute to power generation. Aggregated zonal find is formed by summing all wind power generated within buses of that zone. Thermal generators refer to generators which must satisfy the deficit between consumer demand and renewable power generated. This deficit is referred to as net load. The OPF solution focuses solely on minimizing the cost of thermal power generation subject to some net load on the grid.

All zones have some distributions which model their inherent variability:

  • Zonal load, Li, follows a symmetric truncated normal distribution. Given some mean and standard deviation, {μ,σ} , a distribution follows a normal distribution. But, by bounding this distribution with some lower bound and upper bound, {l,r}, the distribution follows a truncated normal distribution. Note, for simplicity, is is assumed that the mean of the lower and upper bounds equates to μ.

(1)Li∼N(l,r,σ)

  • Zonal Wind Speed, Wi, follows a two-parameter Weibull distribution. With shape and scale parameters of k and $\omega $, respectively

(2)Wi=W(k,ω)

From these wind speeds, zonal wind power, Ri, is calculated as the sum of wind power across n wind buses in a given zone. w0 represents the threshold wind speed for power generation. wr represents the wind speed which reaches the maximum allowable wind power capacity. For this work, it is assumed that 100 MW is the maximum allowable wind power capacity for an individual bus, w0=1 m/s, wr=15 m/s.

(3)Ri,k={0MW,Wi<w0100 MW (Wi3−w03)wr3−Wi3,Wi∈[w0,wr]100MW,Wi>wr

(4)Ri=∑k=1nRi,k

  • Zonal Net Load, Ni, is calculated as the difference of Li and Wi.

These conventions of calculation are outlined in Hong and Apolinario 2021 (Uncertainty in unit commitment in power systems: A review of models, methods, and applications).

Reserve Calculation

From Zhang et al. 2023, reserve, Θ, is calculated:

(5)Θ=max[∑i∈νTPi,maxT−(∑i∈νLPiL−∑i∈νRPiR),0]

Summing across the sets of thermal generators (νT), load buses (νL), or renewable buses (νR), the reserve is calculated as the thermal generation capacity system system-wide subtracted by the net load system-wide. Reserve requirements are violated if: Θ<MRR for a system or zone. For the following work, the MRR is set to 500 MW and the maximum thermal generation capacity is set to 600 MW.

# Set maximum thermal generation
max_thermal_gen = 600 # Megawatts

# Initialize an empty list
reserve_array = []

# Loop over each zone to compute and store reserves
for i in range(1, num_zones + 1):
    # Compute reserve
    reserve_value = (
        max_thermal_gen * np.sum(globals()[f'zone{i}_gen_bidx'] & ~globals()[f'zone{i}_wind_bidx'])
        - (
            np.sum(p_load_data[globals()[f'zone{i}_load_bidx']], axis=0)
            - np.sum(p_gen_data[globals()[f'zone{i}_wind_bidx']], axis=0)
        )
    )

    # Ensure reserve_value is a row vector (1, 1000)
    reserve_array.append(reserve_value.reshape(1, -1))

# Convert list to NumPy array (final shape: 16 x 1000)
# Each row is a zone
# Each column is an observation
reserve_array = np.vstack(reserve_array)

Research Questions

My research question for this project is evaluating to what extent load bus and generation bus counts affect the expected reserve margin. Does a larger count of generator buses affect the extent to which zonal MRR requirements may fail? Is MRR more likely to be violated in a zone of higher load bus count? Are MRR failures more likely to fail in zones with smaller proportions of load generator count to overall generator counts?

One example would be: is zone (A), represented by a proportion of one thermal generator for every three load buses, more likely to experience failure than zone (B), represented by a proportion of one thermal generator for every two load buses? I believe this question to be important as it can define how future grids are built. Would we want a grid with numerous small-scale generators providing power for a similar number of homes or is it better to have a smaller number of large-scale generators servicing numerous communities? By evaluating the proportions and the corresponding likelihood of reserve failure, we can see if any particular system is preferable in its minimization of failure probability.

Hypotheses and Tests

Hypothesis

I believe that zones with higher proportions of thermal generator buses and lower proportions of load buses will be most effective in mitigating MRR violations. With more prevalent thermal generative buses and fewer load buses, it is expected that these zones will be able to reduce burden on each generator and allow for a larger amount of unused capacity.

Methods

I will consider three inputs to reserve margin - zonal thermal proportions, zonal load proportions, and zonal thermal-load proportions. Zonal thermal proportions refer to the number of thermal buses divided by the total number of buses in the zone. Zonal load proportions refer to the number of load buses divided by the total number of buses in the zone. And zonal thermal-load proportions refer to the number of thermal buses divided by the number of load buses in the zone. These proportions are shown in the table below.

Results and Discussion

The above correlations show minimal impact between the aforementioned proportions and the probabilities of MRR violation. Thus, there does not exist any statistically significant relation between the prevalence of zonal features and reserve failure for the given DC OPF data. It is known that the zonal reserve margin is dependent upon the grid’s inputs of load, renewable generation, and thermal generation. As such, it is expected that distribution parameters of load and renewable energy production (originally wind speed) would display much greater impact on the probability of failure than the prevalence of bus assignments.

Conclusions

From this work I learned that risk analysis cannot be performed without an analysis into the parameters or hyperparameters of a simulation. Although there exist assignments of power generation and load which describe a grid’s zone and its features, these features alone cannot be used to quantify probabilities of failure by MRR violation. This result may be used to show that the comparison of systems (A) and (B) presented in the research question cannot be answered without understanding the needs of an individual zone or grid at-large.

In future work, I would like to (a) work further in zonal assignments and (b) evaluate other risks in the grid. As was mentioned in the discussion of the Zhang et al. 2023 paper, the geographic partitioning of a grid yields arbitrary boundaries. These zones are used solely for the assignment of parameters with no consideration of the underlying topology or known information pertaining to this grid (as would be outlined in Josz et al. 2016). Alternate slicings of the zones apart from the radial fashion selected could show some impact on the power flow by altering which regions are at most risk of MRR violation or seeing how the grid reacts to new regions of higher demand.

In this work, I only discussed one kind of risk in the maintenance of reserve margin. This work does not consider other necessary evaluations of transmission line overflows nor load shedding. I believe that were I to have another opportunity to present this work, I would like to evaluate risks in transmission by zone to consider if these proportions play a role in straining allowable line flows.
